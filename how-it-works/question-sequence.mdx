The coach covers the full YC application across 11 sections, each one a distinct area YC partners evaluate when deciding who to interview. The sections build on each other — your Company Description shapes how reviewers read your Traction, and your Idea Origin backstory gives weight to your Competitors insight claim. Working through them in order helps you notice where your answers are inconsistent before a reviewer does. That said, you can start with any section that's most pressing for you right now.

## The 11-section sequence

<Steps>
  <Step title="Company Description">
    **"Describe what your company does in 50 characters or less."**

    The hardest constraint in the application. Fifty characters forces you to cut every qualifier and pick the one thing that actually matters. The coach pushes you to say what you do — not what category you're in, not what technology you use, and not what problem exists in the world.

    Covered in depth in the [Company Description guide](/yc-guide/company-description).
  </Step>

  <Step title="Product Description">
    **"What is your company going to make?"**

    More room to breathe, but a narrower question than it looks. YC wants to understand the specific product, who uses it, and what it does for them — not a vision statement. The coach checks whether your answer describes what you've built or what you plan to build, and pushes hard on the difference.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Team">
    **"Who writes code, or does other technical work on your product?"** and **"Are you looking for a cofounder?"**

    Two distinct questions. The first asks the coach to assess technical coverage on your founding team — who actually builds the thing. The second surfaces cofounder gaps honestly. The coach probes for evidence of founder-market fit: not just credentials, but relevant lived experience with the problem.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Location">
    **"Where do you live now, and where would the company be based after YC?"** and **"Explain your decision regarding location."**

    Straightforward factual answers, but the rationale matters. If you're not planning to relocate to the Bay Area, you need a clear reason. The coach helps you state your location situation plainly without hedging.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Progress">
    **"How far along are you?"** plus time commitment, tech stack, and prior programs.

    One of the most evidence-heavy sections. The coach asks what you've actually shipped — not what's on your roadmap — and checks your git history and any available project files to ground the answer in reality. Time commitment questions often reveal whether this is a full-time project or a side experiment.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Traction">
    **"Are people using your product?"** and **"Do you have revenue?"**

    The section most founders struggle with. The coach won't accept top-line numbers without retention and engagement context. "10,000 users" means nothing without knowing how many were active last week. The coach asks for real numbers over defined time periods, and helps you state pre-revenue status honestly if that's where you are.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Idea Origin">
    **"Why did you pick this idea to work on? Do you have domain expertise? How do you know people need what you're making?"**

    Three interlinked questions that together ask: why you, why this, and how do you know? The coach probes for a lived-pain story or documented domain experience — not "I saw a gap in the market." If you've talked to customers, this is where those notes become essential.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Competitors">
    **"Who are your competitors? What do you understand about your business that they don't?"**

    The second question is the real test. Listing competitors is easy. Articulating a specific insight advantage — one sentence about what you know that they've missed — is where most applications are weakest. The coach won't accept "there are no direct competitors" and helps you identify indirect substitutes and name your genuine edge.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Monetization">
    **"How do or will you make money? How much could you make?"**

    The coach distinguishes between current revenue, a credible near-term model, and pure speculation. Revenue projections are not traction. If you're pre-revenue, the coach helps you name the buyer, the budget they'd use, and comparable products they already pay for.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Equity">
    **"Have you formed ANY legal entity yet?"** plus **"Have you taken any investment?"** and **"Are you currently fundraising?"**

    Factual questions with specific required answers. The coach helps you state your legal and funding situation clearly without over- or under-disclosing.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Curious">
    **"What convinced you to apply to Y Combinator?"** and **"How did you hear about Y Combinator?"**

    The final section. Often treated as an afterthought — the coach makes sure it isn't. Your answer to "what convinced you" signals self-awareness about where you are as a company and what you actually want from YC. Generic answers ("YC is the best accelerator") are a missed opportunity.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Info>
  You can start with any section or work through them in order. Your progress is saved after each confirmed answer, so you can stop mid-session and resume exactly where you left off. The coach keeps the full context of your confirmed answers across sections to check for consistency before generating the final draft.
</Info>

<Note>
  Question wording shifts between YC batches. The questions listed here reflect the current application form, but always verify the exact text against the live YC application at [ycombinator.com/apply](https://www.ycombinator.com/apply) before submitting your answers.
</Note>
