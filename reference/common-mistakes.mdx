---
title: "Common YC Application Mistakes and How to Fix Them"
sidebarTitle: "Common Mistakes"
description: "The patterns that get otherwise strong applications rejected — vague traction claims, top-down TAM, belief language. Learn to spot and fix each one."
---

These patterns come from hundreds of YC applications reviewed by YC alumni through YC Roaster. Fixing even a few of them dramatically improves your application's signal-to-noise ratio.

<Warning>
  The coach will flag every one of these patterns and push back until you address them.
</Warning>

<AccordionGroup>

  <Accordion title="We believe X without evidence">
    **The mistake:** Phrases like "we believe," "we think," "we feel," and "we expect" are assertions dressed up as insights. They appear constantly in weak applications and signal that you're substituting confidence for evidence.

    **Why it hurts:** YC partners read thousands of applications. Belief-language is a pattern match for founders who haven't done the work to verify their assumptions. It doesn't matter how confidently you state it — if there's no evidence behind the claim, it reads as a gap.

    **How to fix it:** Replace every belief-statement with the evidence that produced it. "We believe the market is ready" becomes "Three enterprise buyers told us their current solution breaks down at scale — here are the specifics." If you can't find the evidence, that's the real problem to solve before submitting.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Top-down TAM without bottom-up math">
    **The mistake:** Citing a market-research figure — "the global supply chain software market is $19.3 trillion" — without connecting it to your specific product, price, and buyer.

    **Why it hurts:** A TAM number from a report tells reviewers nothing about how much of that market you can actually reach. It signals you haven't done the hard thinking about who your first hundred customers are.

    **How to fix it:** Build the math from the bottom up. Identify your specific buyer profile, count how many of them exist, multiply by your price, and state the result. For example: "There are 80,000 mid-market manufacturers in the US. 20% have the pain we solve. At $12K ACV, our reachable market is ~$190M." That's a market argument. A report citation is not.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Vanity metrics without usage context">
    **The mistake:** Presenting top-line numbers — signups, waitlist size, impression counts — without the denominators and retention data that give them meaning.

    **Why it hurts:** Experienced reviewers immediately look for the denominator. "10,000 users" is meaningless without knowing how many were active last week, what "active" means for your product, and what the 30-day retention rate is. Vanity metrics without context read as an attempt to obscure weak underlying numbers.

    **How to fix it:** Lead with the metric that's hardest to fake: retention. "47% of users who activated in week 1 returned in week 4" is a strong signal. "10,000 signups" is not. If you don't have retention data yet, say so honestly — that's more credible than a number that doesn't hold up to one follow-up question.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Users love the product without retention data">
    **The mistake:** Offering sentiment as evidence of product quality without any behavioral data to back it up.

    **Why it hurts:** "Love" is unfalsifiable. It's easy to believe your users love your product when you're the one talking to them. What reviewers want is behavioral proof: do they come back? How often? What do they do when they return?

    **How to fix it:** Replace sentiment claims with usage numbers. "Our users love the product" becomes "Our 30-day retention is 68% and our average user logs in 4 times per week." If you don't have those numbers yet, describe the qualitative signal specifically: quote a user verbatim, describe a behavior you observed, or reference a specific moment where someone told you the product was essential to their work.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Future tense when the question asks what you have done">
    **The mistake:** Answering questions about current progress with future plans, especially in the traction and progress sections.

    **Why it hurts:** "We plan to launch in Q3" is not an answer to "how far along are you?" It tells reviewers your most credible signal is a future event that hasn't happened. That's the definition of pre-traction, and it's fine to say so — but hiding it behind plans is worse than just being direct.

    **How to fix it:** Answer in the present tense. What exists today? What shipped last week? What is a real user doing with your product right now? If the honest answer is "we launched in March, we have 3 paying customers at $500/month, and we shipped [feature] last week" — that's a great answer. Be specific about what's real.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Founder-market fit asserted without a lived-pain story">
    **The mistake:** Claiming you're "uniquely positioned" to solve a problem without explaining how you came to know that problem better than anyone else.

    **Why it hurts:** "We have deep domain expertise in logistics" is a claim, not evidence. Reviewers have read it in hundreds of applications. What they're looking for is the specific experience — the role, the project, the failure, the edge case — that gave you insight competitors don't have.

    **How to fix it:** Tell the story. "I spent 4 years as a dispatcher at a regional trucking company. I built the manual process this software replaces. I know every edge case." That's founder-market fit demonstrated. "We have 40 years of combined experience in logistics" is founder-market fit asserted. One gets you through; one doesn't.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Competitor section listing only incumbents with no insight advantage">
    **The mistake:** Naming well-known competitors and stopping there, without explaining what you understand about the problem that they've missed.

    **Why it hurts:** A list of incumbents tells a reviewer you've done a Google search. It doesn't tell them why your product will win. Worse, it often signals that you've listed the obvious players as a defensive move and haven't thought carefully about the real competitive landscape.

    **How to fix it:** Name the real competitors including indirect substitutes — spreadsheets, consultants, doing nothing — and then make a specific insight claim. What do you know about why the incumbents' approaches fail? What did you discover by talking to customers that explains why the existing solutions leave money on the table? That's the answer reviewers want.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Revenue projections presented as market validation">
    **The mistake:** Listing projected revenue figures — "we expect to reach $1M ARR by end of year" — in a traction or market-validation context.

    **Why it hurts:** Projections are models. Models are assumptions. Presenting a projection as evidence of demand reads as: "we have no real traction, so here's a number we made up." Reviewers see this constantly and it is one of the fastest ways to lose credibility.

    **How to fix it:** Separate current reality from projections and lead with reality. "We're at $8K MRR with 15% MoM growth" is a real signal. "At that rate we'd reach $100K MRR in 18 months" is context for the real signal — not the signal itself. If you're pre-revenue, say so, and lead with the demand evidence you do have: LOIs, design partner agreements, price validation conversations.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="One-off anecdotes substituted for usage patterns">
    **The mistake:** Using a single strong data point — a Fortune 500 company expressing interest, one user who loves the product — as if it represents a pattern.

    **Why it hurts:** YC is betting on repeatable businesses. One excited customer tells you almost nothing about whether a second, third, and hundredth customer will come. A single Fortune 500 "expressing interest" is not a sale, and reviewers know the difference.

    **How to fix it:** Look for the pattern, not the outlier. "We have 6 active design partners, 2 of whom have agreed to convert to paid at $2K/month when we GA" is a pattern. "A VP at Google told us this is exactly what they need" is an anecdote. If you only have the anecdote, be honest — and explain what you're doing to turn one signal into a repeatable one.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Deferring monetization without a credible reason">
    **The mistake:** Deferring the monetization question without a credible reason — no comparable business model, no named buyer, no sense of what the unit economics will look like.

    **Why it hurts:** Without a monetization thesis, "we'll figure it out later" reads as "we haven't thought about this." The concern isn't that you don't have revenue yet — pre-revenue companies get into YC all the time. The concern is that you don't have a coherent answer to who will pay, what they'll pay, and why.

    **How to fix it:** Even if you haven't charged anyone, name the buyer, the budget they're using, and the comparable products they already pay for. "We're not charging yet, but our buyer is a VP of Operations who currently pays $8K/year for [comparable tool]. We've told 6 potential customers our price will be $12K/year and none of them objected." That's a monetization thesis. "We'll figure it out" is not.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="There are no direct competitors">
    **The mistake:** Claiming the competitive landscape is empty or that your product is so novel it has no competition.

    **Why it hurts:** This is almost never true, and it signals poor market research. Every product competes with something — at minimum, it competes with the status quo: a spreadsheet, a manual process, a consultant, or doing nothing. Saying "no competitors" makes a reviewer wonder what else you're missing about the market.

    **How to fix it:** Name the real competition, including indirect substitutes. Then make a specific insight claim about what those alternatives have missed. "Our customers currently use a combination of Salesforce and spreadsheets. The failure mode is [specific thing]. We solve it by [specific mechanism]." That answer is more credible and more compelling than an empty competitive landscape.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Generic go-to-market with no real acquisition motion">
    **The mistake:** Describing your GTM as "SEO, paid ads, word of mouth, and social media" without any evidence of what actually works.

    **Why it hurts:** Every weak application lists these channels. They're not a strategy — they're a list of tools that exist. Reviewers want to know your specific acquisition motion: who exactly you're selling to, how you reach them, what happens when you do, and what the conversion rate looks like.

    **How to fix it:** Describe your actual motion with numbers. "Our first 20 customers came through direct outbound to [specific persona] on LinkedIn. Our reply rate is 14% and our qualified-call-to-close rate is 30%. We've identified [specific next channel] as repeatable because [specific reason]." If you haven't found a working channel yet, say so and describe what you've tried and what you're testing next.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Moat claims without a described mechanism">
    **The mistake:** Asserting a competitive moat — "our data advantage," "our proprietary AI," "our network effects" — without explaining the mechanism that makes it defensible.

    **Why it hurts:** "Our data moat means competitors can't catch up" is a claim, not an argument. Reviewers immediately ask: why can't they catch up? What specifically does more data unlock? How long would it take a well-funded competitor to replicate it?

    **How to fix it:** Describe the mechanism. "Every customer's data trains a personalized model for that customer. Competitors starting today would need 2+ years of customer data to match our recommendation accuracy on new accounts." Now the moat is explained. A claim without a mechanism is just marketing copy.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Long answers that never answer the question">
    **The mistake:** Writing 400-word answers that provide a lot of context, background, and reasoning — but never directly address what YC asked.

    **Why it hurts:** Word count is not a substitute for substance. Reviewers flag long non-answers as a sign of fuzzy thinking or an attempt to obscure a weak answer behind volume. A 100-word answer that directly responds to the question is better than a 500-word answer that circles around it.

    **How to fix it:** Use the underline test. Read your answer and underline the sentence that directly answers the question. If you can't find one, rewrite. Lead with the direct answer, then add supporting detail. Never bury the answer in context.
  </Accordion>

</AccordionGroup>
