These patterns were distilled from hundreds of successful YC applications reviewed through YC Roaster. They're not templates — they're structural qualities that strong answers share. You can't copy them, but you can internalize them and use them to pressure-test every sentence you write.

<Note>
  The "survives the interview" test: every claim in your application should be something you can elaborate on in a 10-minute YC interview without notes. If a sentence sounds smooth but you can't back it up off the cuff, cut it or replace it with something you can actually defend.
</Note>

## Specificity over claims

The single strongest pattern in successful applications is **evidence over assertion**. Every claim is backed by a number, a named customer, a specific behavior, or a verifiable fact.

Strong answers use specificity at every level:

- "We have 10 paying customers at $500/month with 90% retention" beats "we're seeing strong traction"
- "We talked to 40 potential customers over 6 weeks; 8 asked us to build it and 3 agreed to be design partners" beats "we've validated demand"
- "47% of users who activated in week 1 returned in week 4" beats "users love the product"
- "3 enterprise customers at $2K/month, signed in the last 6 weeks" beats "we have enterprise interest"

Numbers with context beat numbers without context. "10X growth" is weaker than "10X growth from $2K to $20K MRR over 4 months." The denominator and the time window are part of the claim. Leave them out and reviewers fill them in with the worst-case assumption.

<Tip>
  You don't need to name customers publicly. "A top-5 US bank" or "a $2B logistics company" is specific enough to be credible without violating NDAs.
</Tip>

## Founder voice

Strong YC applications sound like they were written by a human being who built something and lived the problem. They don't sound like startup pitch decks or blog posts.

What founder voice looks like in practice:

- Active voice: "we built X" not "X was built by our team"
- Past tense for what's done, present tense for what's running, future tense sparingly and always tied to a specific milestone
- Confidence without hedging: state what you know, acknowledge what you don't, rather than softening everything with "we believe" and "we think"
- Specific language over jargon: "mid-market logistics companies with 50–200 drivers who lose $X/month to route inefficiency" instead of "enterprise customers in the supply chain vertical"

The test: read your application answer out loud. If it sounds like a person talking about something they built and care about, it has founder voice. If it sounds like it could have been written about any startup in your category, rewrite it.

## Evidence-first structure

In strong applications, every section leads with evidence. Background, context, and reasoning come after the proof, not before it.

This matters most in four places:

**Traction:** Lead with the metric that's hardest to fake. For post-launch companies, that's retention and revenue growth. For pre-launch, it's named customer commitments and behavioral signals from prototype testing.

**Market:** Lead with your specific buyer and what they pay for now. The TAM figure, if you include it, is supporting context — not the argument itself.

**Team:** Lead with the most relevant thing you've shipped and what happened. Credentials and backgrounds are context; proof of building is the evidence.

**Competitors:** Lead with what you understand about why existing solutions fail. The competitive landscape is context; your insight is the argument.

<Info>
  Strong applications use concrete evidence throughout — not just in the traction section. Your product description, market analysis, team background, and competition answer should all be grounded in specific, checkable facts.
</Info>

## Bottom-up market thinking

Strong applications build market arguments from the ground up, not the top down.

The pattern: identify your specific buyer, count how many exist, multiply by your price, and show the result. Then explain why those buyers don't have a good solution today and how you'll reach them first.

What this looks like:

- "There are 50,000 [ICPs] in the US. We can reach 10% of them through [specific channel]. At $5K ACV, that's a $25M initial market — and if we expand to [adjacent segment], it becomes $200M."
- "Our buyer is a VP of Operations at a mid-market manufacturer. There are 80,000 of them in the US. They currently pay $8K/year for [incumbent]. We're cheaper and solve a problem the incumbent doesn't."

The why-now argument matters too. Strong applications ground the market timing in a real change: a regulatory shift, a technology that just became available, a behavior change that opened the wedge. "Why hasn't this been built before?" is a question every reviewer is asking. Answer it directly.

## Honest gap acknowledgment

Counter-intuitively, acknowledging what you don't have makes strong applications more credible, not less.

Pre-revenue is fine — just say so, and explain what you have instead. No product analytics yet? Say so, and describe the qualitative signal you do have. One co-founder short? Name it and explain the plan.

What makes this work is pairing the gap with what you're doing about it. "We don't have retention data yet because we launched two weeks ago. Our proxy is that 3 of our first 5 users have already messaged us asking about features — unprompted." That's an honest answer that still conveys signal.

The failure mode is papering over gaps with vague confidence. "Our retention is strong" when you don't have retention data is worse than saying you don't have the data yet. Reviewers know what a two-week-old product looks like. Pretending you have metrics you don't have damages trust across the whole application.

## Insight-based competition

Strong applications don't list competitors and describe how you're different in a feature comparison table. They make a claim about what incumbents have missed — and why your specific insight lets you solve it in a way they can't.

The structure:

1. Name the real alternatives, including indirect substitutes (spreadsheets, manual processes, doing nothing)
2. Explain the specific failure mode of those alternatives — not in general terms, but the exact thing that breaks down
3. Claim the insight that lets you solve that failure mode
4. Explain why incumbents are structurally unable to replicate it

For example: "Our customers currently use [Incumbent A] plus spreadsheets. The failure mode is [specific thing that breaks at scale]. Incumbents know about it but can't fix it because [structural reason — their data model, their pricing, their customer base]. We solve it by [specific mechanism]."

That answer tells reviewers you understand the competitive landscape, you've talked to customers who've been burned by alternatives, and you have a real insight — not just a feature difference.
