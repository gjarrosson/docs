These two sections separate founders who know their numbers from those who don't. Progress tells YC how fast you're moving and how committed you are. Traction tells them whether the market wants what you're building. Together, they're the closest thing in the application to proof — proof of velocity, proof of conviction, and proof of demand. The most common failure in both sections is the same: substituting future plans for present reality, and substituting top-line numbers for the retention and engagement data that actually matters. Write both sections in present and past tense. What you plan to do belongs in the roadmap, not in the progress and traction answers.

## The exact questions

**Progress:**
> - How far along are you?
> - How long have each of you been working on this? How much of that has been full-time?
> - What tech stack are you using, or planning to use, to build this product?

**Traction:**
> - Are people using your product?
> - Do you have revenue?

---

## Progress: what YC is evaluating

YC uses the progress section to calibrate two things: **velocity** (are you building fast?) and **commitment** (are you fully in?). A team that has been working nights and weekends for two years with nothing to show is a velocity problem and a commitment signal. A team that went full-time three months ago, shipped an MVP, and already has paying customers is the opposite signal. The tech stack question is a reality check — vague or aspirational answers ("we're using cloud-based AI infrastructure") suggest the product isn't built yet.

### What a strong progress answer looks like

<Steps>
  <Step title="Name specific milestones with dates">
    Don't describe the current state in isolation — show the progression. "We started in October. Built the MVP by December. Launched a closed beta in January with 12 design partners. Opened to the public in March." Milestones with dates tell a story of momentum that current state alone cannot.
  </Step>
  <Step title="State what exists today that someone can use">
    Be specific about what is built, deployed, and working right now. "We have a web app in production at [URL]" is better than "we've built a prototype." If there are limitations in the current version, name them — an honest description of a limited but real product beats an inflated description of a more complete but fictional one.
  </Step>
  <Step title="State full-time commitment with a specific date">
    "Both co-founders went full-time on March 1st" is what YC wants to see. "We're working on this as much as we can" is not. If one or both founders are still at a job, say when that changes and what the trigger is. Part-time is survivable; unexplained part-time is a red flag.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Name your actual tech stack">
    List the language, framework, database, and hosting provider you're using today. "Next.js frontend, Postgres on Supabase, deployed on Railway; Python FastAPI for the ML backend" is a strong answer. "Modern AI frameworks with a cloud-based architecture" signals that the product isn't built yet. The specificity of your tech stack answer is a proxy for how real your product is.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Show what changed in the last 30–60 days">
    Velocity is shown by change, not by state. What did you ship last month? What changed in the product since three months ago? "We added two-way Slack integration in April and shipped a new analytics dashboard in May" is a stronger signal than describing the current feature set without a timeline.
  </Step>
</Steps>

### Common mistakes in the progress section

<Warning>
  **"We're still building the MVP"** — This tells a reviewer nothing about what's been done. What percentage is built? What's working? What are you waiting on? If the MVP isn't done, describe what's done and when the MVP ships.
</Warning>

<Warning>
  **Part-time with no commitment plan** — "We've been working on this nights and weekends for two years" is a significant red flag. Two years of part-time work with no launch is a velocity problem. If you're still part-time, explain the specific trigger that will move you to full-time and when that happens.
</Warning>

<Warning>
  **Vague tech stack answers** — "We're using modern AI technologies and cloud infrastructure" tells reviewers the product probably isn't built. Name everything. If you're using OpenAI's API, say so. If you're on Vercel, say so. The specificity is the point.
</Warning>

<Warning>
  **"We plan to" when the question asks what you've done** — The progress section asks about the present, not the future. "We plan to launch in Q3 and onboard our first paying customers by Q4" is an answer to a different question. What exists today?
</Warning>

<Warning>
  **The "almost ready" trap** — "Our MVP is almost ready" is one of the weakest phrases in a YC application. "Almost ready" means not ready. Describe what *is* working and usable today — even if it's a limited version — rather than describing the complete version that isn't done yet.
</Warning>

## Traction: what YC is evaluating

Traction is the highest-signal section of most applications. It's the closest thing to proof that the market wants what you're building and that this team can execute. YC reviewers have seen thousands of applications and they know exactly what real traction looks like versus vanity metrics. Their goal in this section is to find evidence of **pull from the world** — customers coming to you, using the product repeatedly, and paying or willing to pay.

### What strong traction looks like

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="For post-launch companies">
    Strong traction answers are precise, include denominators, and show direction — not just current state.

    - **Active user numbers with definitions:** "87 active users, defined as users who ran at least one analysis in the last 7 days" — not just "87 users"
    - **Retention signal:** "80% of users who activated in month 1 returned in month 3" or "30-day retention is 62%"
    - **Revenue with growth rate:** "We're at $8K MRR with 15% month-over-month growth over the last 4 months" — not just "$8K MRR"
    - **Paying customer specifics:** how many customers, what they pay, when they signed, how they found you
    - **Honest churn disclosure:** if churn is high, say so and say what you know about why — this is more credible than hiding it
    - **Organic pull signals:** word-of-mouth, inbound that converts, referral loops — these signal product-market fit more than paid acquisition does

    The before/after from real traction answers makes the difference clear:

    > **Weak:** "We're seeing strong early traction with thousands of users interested in our product."

    > **Strong:** "We launched in February. We have 87 active users — defined as users who ran at least one analysis in the last 7 days. 22 are paying $49/month. MoM growth is 34% over the last 3 months. Churn is 8% monthly — we know this is high and have identified the root cause as onboarding friction; we shipped a new onboarding flow last week."

    The strong answer names the metric and its definition, gives a paying customer count with a price, gives a growth rate with a time period, names the churn rate, and acknowledges the problem while describing what's being done about it.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="For pre-launch companies">
    Pre-launch companies get into YC. What they need is evidence that the market wants the product before it exists, and signals that real demand is coming.

    - **Named customer discovery with numbers:** "We talked to 40 potential customers at [specific types of companies] over 6 weeks. 31 of them described the same core problem. 8 asked us to build this and 3 agreed to be design partners." This is a pattern — not an anecdote.
    - **Credible demand commitments:** signed letters of intent, deposits, design partner agreements. Even draft versions signal credibility.
    - **Prototype or concept testing with behavioral data:** "We showed 10 target users a prototype. 8 completed the core task in under 2 minutes. Average session length was 14 minutes — they kept exploring."
    - **Waitlist conversion signals:** not just how many people signed up but what percentage took the next meaningful action.

    The thing to avoid: "We have 5,000 waitlist signups" with no conversion data. A waitlist number without a conversion rate is not traction evidence. What percentage of those signups activated, invited a friend, or agreed to pay?
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

### The vanity metrics trap

The single most common traction mistake is leading with top-line numbers that sound impressive but carry no information about whether the product is working.

<Info>
  **Numbers without denominators mislead experienced reviewers.** "10,000 users" out of how many signups? What do they do in the product? How often? What percentage came back in week 4? "1 million impressions" from an ad campaign doesn't tell you whether anyone cared. "2,000 waitlist signups" without a conversion rate tells you about your marketing, not your product.

  The denominator and the retention curve matter as much as the top-line number. If you have 10,000 signups but your week-4 retention is 2%, that's a leak, not traction.
</Info>

Common vanity metrics and what to replace them with:

| **Vanity metric** | **What to say instead** |
|---|---|
| "10,000 users" | "10,000 signups; 1,200 have activated (defined as completing the core action); 600 returned in week 3" |
| "Strong early traction" | "22 paying customers at $49/month; 15% MoM growth over 4 months" |
| "Partnerships in progress" | Name who, what stage, and what's agreed — or don't mention it |
| "Revenue projections of $1M ARR" | "$8K MRR; at 15% MoM growth that would be ~$96K MRR in 18 months" |
| "Our beta users love it" | "NPS of 62; 4 of our 15 beta users referred at least one other user within 30 days" |

### Common mistakes in the traction section

<Warning>
  **Projections presented as traction** — Revenue projections are a model. They are a set of assumptions about the future. YC is looking for proof of current demand. "We expect to reach $1M ARR by end of year" reads as: we have no real traction. If you want to include a projection, anchor it in actual current data: "We're at $8K MRR with 15% MoM growth. At that rate we'd reach roughly $100K MRR in 18 months."
</Warning>

<Warning>
  **One-off anecdotes substituted for patterns** — "A VP at Google told us this is exactly what they need" is not traction. One executive expressing interest is not a buying signal. YC looks for repeatable patterns across multiple independent buyers. "We have 6 active design partners, 2 of whom have already agreed to convert to paid at $2K/month when we GA" is a pattern.
</Warning>

<Warning>
  **Hiding behind top-line numbers** — If your top-line user or signup number is strong but retention is poor, disclosing that and explaining what you're doing about it is substantially better than burying the retention number. Reviewers are looking for self-awareness as much as performance. A founder who says "our 30-day retention is 35% and we know why and here's what we shipped to fix it" looks better than a founder who leads with "10K users" and doesn't mention retention.
</Warning>

### The revenue question specifically

For the "do you have revenue?" question, state your current MRR or ARR and your month-over-month growth rate. These two numbers together tell the story.

- **If you have revenue:** "We're at $12K MRR, up from $0 in October. 15% MoM growth over the last 4 months. 8 customers, all monthly subscriptions at $1,500/month."
- **If you're pre-revenue:** Say so clearly and explain when you expect to have revenue and what has to happen first. "We're pre-revenue. We have 3 design partners who have agreed to a $1K/month contract when we hit GA, which we expect in 6 weeks."

<Note>
  If you have no traction yet, say so honestly. Pre-traction companies get into YC — especially when the team is strong, the problem is clear, and the market is real. What you need is a credible reason why traction is coming: evidence from customer conversations, demand commitments, or prototype testing that shows the market wants what you're building. What you cannot do is pretend traction exists when it doesn't. Reviewers know the difference, and an inflated traction answer is worse than an honest "we're pre-launch."
</Note>
