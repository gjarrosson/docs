These two questions sit at the core of the YC application. Together, they ask YC to believe three things: that you understand the problem more deeply than anyone else, that real people actually need what you're building, and that you see something in the market that existing players have missed. Get these right and you've answered the most important question on the form — why you, why this, why now.

## The Questions

1. **"Why did you pick this idea to work on? Do you have domain expertise in this area? How do you know people need what you're making?"**
2. **"Who are your competitors? What do you understand about your business that they don't?"**

---

## Idea Origin: Why You Picked This

YC is not asking you to summarize a market opportunity. They're asking why *you* — specifically — chose this problem. The strongest answers come from one of three places: you lived the problem firsthand, you built something adjacent to it and developed rare insight, or you've spent enough time with the people who have the problem that you understand it as well as they do.

A team that "identified a gap in the market" is meaningfully weaker than a team that was frustrated by this problem for years and finally decided to fix it themselves. The former describes an exercise in market research. The latter describes conviction.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Tell a specific origin story">
    Don't say "we saw an opportunity." Say what actually happened. Walk through the specific moment, project, or frustration that led you here. The more concrete and personal the story, the more credible your conviction.

    **Weak:** "We identified a gap in the compliance automation market after researching the space extensively."

    **Strong:** "I spent 3 years as a security engineer at a Series B startup. We went through our first SOC2 audit and I personally spent 4 months collecting evidence — pulling screenshots, chasing down engineers for access logs, rewriting policy docs three times. We spent $80K on an auditor and still failed the first time. I quit to build the tool I wished existed."
  </Step>
  <Step title="Ground domain expertise in work, not credentials">
    Credentials establish context. Proof of building — and specifically, proof that you've operated in this space and encountered this problem directly — is what actually matters. What did you build? What did you learn that someone reading about this industry for a week wouldn't know?

    **Weak:** "Our team has 20+ years of combined experience in enterprise software."

    **Strong:** "My co-founder was the person at [company] who submitted evidence to auditors for four consecutive SOC2 cycles. She built the internal tooling that automated the process. She's written production code for 8 years and she knows every edge case because she hit them herself."

    Note the difference: the second answer names a specific role, a specific task, a specific outcome, and a specific duration. It tells you something real about why this person understands the problem.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Prove demand with evidence, not belief">
    The second half of this question — "how do you know people need what you're making?" — is a demand validation question. YC wants evidence of need, not conviction about need.

    The hierarchy of evidence, from strongest to weakest:
    - Paying customers
    - Signed LOIs or design partner agreements
    - People who asked you to build this before you started
    - Structured customer discovery with named findings (not just "we talked to people")
    - Survey data (weakest — people lie on surveys)

    Name specific people, numbers, and what they actually said. "We talked to 35 security engineers at companies that did SOC2 in the last year — 31 of them described the same 4-month evidence collection nightmare" is a finding. "We validated demand with potential customers" is not.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Warning>
  "We believe the market is ready for this" is one of the most common phrases in weak applications. Any answer that leads with what you *believe*, *think*, or *feel* without backing evidence signals that you're substituting conviction for proof. Replace every belief statement with the evidence that produced it — or acknowledge you don't have evidence yet and explain what you're doing to get it.
</Warning>

## Domain Expertise: Demonstrating, Not Asserting

Founder-market fit is one of YC's most important signals. They're trying to answer a simple question: does this team have an advantage over the next five teams who could theoretically build the same thing?

The answer can't be "we have deep expertise." That's a claim. The answer has to be the backstory that *produces* the claim — the specific years, the specific role, the specific lesson learned that is counterintuitive and comes only from being inside the problem.

Ask yourself: what do you know about this problem that someone reading a TechCrunch article about your industry wouldn't know? What did you learn the hard way? What did you get wrong before you got it right? Those are the answers YC wants to hear. Lead with them.

<Accordion title="What if your domain expertise is thin?">
  If your connection to the problem is primarily through research rather than lived experience, be honest about it — and then show how you've compensated. Extensive structured customer discovery (30+ interviews with target buyers, documented findings, recurring patterns) can substitute for personal experience if you've done it rigorously. What you can't do is claim expertise you don't have. If your only connection to the problem is that you "saw a market opportunity," that's what you should say — and then spend the rest of the answer making the case that the customer evidence you've gathered fills the gap.
</Accordion>

## Competitors: There Are Always Competitors

The competitors question is where many founders damage their application by saying the wrong thing immediately. The single most penalized answer in the entire application is "we have no direct competitors." Almost nothing signals poor market research more clearly — and that uncertainty spreads to everything else you've claimed.

Every product competes with something. At minimum, your product competes with:
- What customers do *today* to solve the problem (even if that's a spreadsheet, a consultant, or a manual process)
- The option to do nothing
- Incumbent tools in adjacent categories

Name them all. Naming real competitors is not a weakness — it's proof that you understand the market.

<Warning>
  Saying "there are no competitors" immediately signals to YC that you don't understand your market. It will put a reader on high alert for everything else you've claimed. Name the real competitive landscape, including indirect substitutes, and then explain your insight.
</Warning>

### The Insight Advantage: What Competitors Have Missed

The second half — "what do you understand about your business that they don't?" — is where most founders leave the most points on the table. They name competitors correctly and then answer the insight question with a feature list: "we have better UX, faster onboarding, and more competitive pricing."

That's a positioning statement, not an insight.

A real insight is structural. It explains why a segment of buyers is systematically underserved by every existing solution, and why that's durable — why the incumbents can't simply copy your approach and close the gap.

<Tip>
  One test for a real insight: can you complete this sentence?

  *"[Competitors] optimize for [X], but our customers actually care about [Y] — and we're the only ones building for [Y] because [structural reason]."*

  If you can't fill in all three blanks with specific, defensible claims, you're not there yet.
</Tip>

<Steps>
  <Step title="Name the real competitors">
    Include named software products, the manual process customers use today, and any indirect substitutes. If your potential customers currently use spreadsheets or hire consultants, say so. Those are competitors.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Describe what they're optimized for">
    Before you can explain what they've missed, you need to explain what they've built for. Who is their target buyer? What problem are they solving for that buyer? What constraints does their business model create?

    **Example:** "Vanta and Drata are built for Series A+ companies with dedicated security teams and $15K–$40K annual budgets. Their sales motion requires high ACV to justify CAC, and their onboarding assumes someone with security expertise on the other end."
  </Step>
  <Step title="Name your specific insight">
    Now state what they've missed and why it's durable.

    **Example:** "Pre-Series A companies — fewer than 50 employees, no dedicated security team — need SOC2 to close enterprise deals but can't afford the implementation overhead. Vanta can't easily serve this segment without rebuilding their onboarding and repricing in a way that would cannibalize their existing motion. We built self-serve from the start, priced at $500/month, with setup that takes 2 hours."

    Notice: this isn't "we're cheaper and easier." It's "there's a specific buyer segment that incumbents structurally cannot serve, and here's the mechanism."
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Common Mistakes

<Accordion title='"There are no direct competitors"'>
  Almost never true. Every market has incumbents, adjacent tools, or the option to do nothing. This answer tells YC you haven't done your research — and makes them doubt everything else in your application.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Feature list instead of insight">
  "Better UX, faster onboarding, lower price" is a positioning statement, not a competitive insight. YC is asking what you *understand* that competitors don't — a structural observation about the market, not a product comparison chart.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Domain expertise asserted without backstory">
  "We have deep domain expertise in logistics" is a claim. The backstory that produced the expertise is the evidence. Lead with the specific role, the specific problem you encountered, and the specific thing you learned that others wouldn't know.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title='"We believe X" instead of customer evidence'>
  "We believe companies are underserved in this area" substitutes conviction for proof. Replace belief statements with the specific evidence: how many people did you talk to, what did they say, what did they commit to?
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Vague customer discovery claims">
  "We've talked to potential customers and validated demand" is not evidence. "We talked to 35 security engineers; 31 described the same problem; 3 are design partners paying $500/month" is evidence. Specifics are what make the claim credible.
</Accordion>
