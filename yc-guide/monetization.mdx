The monetization question is one of the clearest tests of whether you've thought about your business or just your product. A strong answer doesn't require you to have revenue. It requires you to demonstrate that you understand who the buyer is, why they would pay, what comparable things they already pay for, and how the math works from the bottom up. YC funds many pre-revenue companies — what they won't fund is a team that has never seriously asked these questions.

## The Question

**"How do or will you make money? How much could you make?"**

---

## What YC Is Actually Evaluating

This question has two parts and they test different things:

**"How do or will you make money?"** tests whether you understand your buyer and your value exchange. YC wants to know: who specifically is the buyer, what budget line does this come from, what do they already pay for that's comparable, and what evidence do you have that they'd pay for yours?

**"How much could you make?"** is an invitation to show bottom-up thinking. It is not asking for a TAM slide. It's asking you to reason from your specific buyer, at your specific price, to a credible market size — starting from the ground up, not from a market research report.

<Note>
  "We'll figure out monetization later" reads as "we haven't thought about this." You don't need a revenue model locked in, but you need a credible hypothesis with a named buyer and a number you can defend. The absence of one signals that you've been building without thinking about whether anyone will pay.
</Note>

## Building a Strong Answer

<Steps>
  <Step title="Name the buyer and the budget">
    Don't say "businesses will pay for this." Say who, specifically, approves the purchase — the job title, the company size, and what budget they're drawing from. A head of security at a 50-person startup has a different budget, approval process, and set of comparables than a VP of Engineering at a 2,000-person enterprise.

    **Weak:** "We'll charge a subscription fee to companies that need compliance automation."

    **Strong:** "We charge $500/month to the person managing compliance at pre-Series A startups — typically the CTO or a senior engineer moonlighting on security. They're already paying for Notion, GitHub, and AWS; this is a line item in the tools budget, not a procurement process."
  </Step>
  <Step title="Anchor your price to something the buyer already pays for">
    Pricing in a vacuum means nothing. Pricing relative to what your buyer already spends — on comparable tools, on the manual process you're replacing, or on the downstream cost of not solving the problem — is immediately legible to a reviewer.

    If your customer currently spends $80K on an auditor and 4 months of engineer time, and you charge $6K/year, that's a clear value story. Name the comparable. Make the math visible.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Show evidence of willingness to pay">
    Evidence of willingness to pay sits on a spectrum. Move as far up this spectrum as you can:

    - **Strongest:** Paying customers with dollar amounts and retention data
    - **Strong:** Signed LOIs with committed dollar amounts
    - **Useful:** Discovery conversations where you named a price and the prospect didn't object, or asked when they could sign up
    - **Weak:** Survey data, "I think people would pay for this," or pricing copied from a competitor without testing it
  </Step>
  <Step title="Build the market size from the bottom up">
    The "how much could you make" question is where TAM slides most often appear and most often fail. A number from a Gartner report does not tell a reviewer whether your specific product, at your specific price, sold to your specific buyer, can reach a meaningful scale.

    Build it from the specific buyer up:

    ```
    Target buyer: Pre-Series A startups needing SOC2 (<100 employees, no security team)
    Estimated count (US): ~40,000 companies at any given time
    Reachable in 3 years: ~5% = 2,000 customers
    ACV: $6,000/year
    3-year addressable opportunity: ~$12M ARR
    Initial market — not the ceiling
    ```

    This answer is less impressive in raw numbers than "$19B compliance market." It is far more credible, and it shows you understand who you're actually selling to.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## If You're Pre-Revenue

Not having revenue yet doesn't disqualify you from answering this question well. What matters is whether you've had real pricing conversations — and what happened.

**If you've quoted a price to potential customers**, describe the conversation. What number did you mention? What was their reaction? Did they ask how to pay? Did they push back? Even an informal "I'd pay $X for this" from a target buyer is meaningful evidence. "I think people would pay for this" is not.

**If you haven't talked about price at all yet**, that's worth knowing — and worth fixing before you submit. Go have three pricing conversations. Ask directly: "If this did X for you, what would you expect to pay per month?" The reaction tells you more than any survey.

<Tip>
  Pre-revenue founders: the question isn't "what will you charge?" It's "what price have you told potential customers you'd charge, and what was their reaction?" The reaction is the evidence. A prospect who says "when can I sign up?" at a quoted price is far more valuable than a projection.
</Tip>

## Common Mistakes

<Accordion title="Deferring monetization until after scale">
  This is occasionally defensible for consumer social products where network effects precede monetization — but even then, you need to explain the mechanism (advertising, premium features, marketplace take rate) and give a comparable. For B2B and most B2C products, this answer signals that you haven't thought about whether anyone will pay. Fix it before submitting.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Revenue projections as market validation">
  Future projections are not evidence of current demand. They are a model made of assumptions. "We expect to reach $1M ARR by end of year" tells a reviewer nothing about whether there's real demand for your product today. It may actually hurt you — it signals you're substituting ambition for proof. Replace projections with what's actually happening: "We're at $8K MRR, 15% MoM growth, 3 months in a row."
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Top-down TAM without bottom-up math">
  "The global compliance software market is $18.5B" is a number from a report. It tells a reviewer almost nothing about whether your specific product can reach meaningful scale. Always pair a market size claim with the bottom-up reasoning: which buyers, at what price, reachable by when, through what motion.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="No named buyer">
  "Companies will pay for this" is not an answer. Name the specific buyer — the job title, the company size, the budget owner. A reviewer who doesn't know who pays for your product can't evaluate whether the price, the motion, or the market size is credible.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Pricing copied from competitors without testing">
  Using a competitor's pricing as your own pricing, without any evidence that your specific buyer would pay that for your specific product, is not a pricing strategy. Your competitor may be selling to a different buyer, through a different motion, with a different value story. Anchor your price to your own conversations and your own value proposition.
</Accordion>

## Pricing Signals That Count

Not all evidence of willingness to pay is equal. Here's how to read what you have:

| Signal | Weight | Why |
|--------|--------|-----|
| Paying customers at stated price | Very strong | Behavior, not intention |
| LOIs with dollar amounts signed | Strong | Committed, not just interested |
| Prospect asked "how do I pay?" when quoted a price | Useful | Buying signal, not just validation |
| Prospect said "I'd pay $X for this" | Useful | Stated preference; holds some weight |
| Prospect said "that sounds reasonable" | Weak | Polite, not signal |
| Survey: "80% would pay for this" | Weak | People lie on surveys |
| "We think customers would pay for this" | None | Assertion, not evidence |

Use the highest-weight signals you have. If you only have weak signals, acknowledge that and explain what you're doing to get stronger ones.
