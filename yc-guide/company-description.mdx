The 50-character company description is the hardest question in your YC application — and the first thing a partner reads. It's not a tagline. It's not a mission statement. It's a precise, complete description of what your product literally does and who it does it for, compressed into 50 characters. That constraint is deliberate: if you can't say what you do in 50 characters, you either don't have a focused enough product or you don't yet understand your own business clearly enough. Getting this right before you write anything else forces the kind of clarity that makes the rest of the application easier to write.

## The exact question

> **Describe what your company does in 50 characters or less.**

## What YC is evaluating

YC partners read hundreds of applications in a short window. The 50-character description is their first filter. They're checking for three things at once:

- **Immediate comprehension** — does a smart person with no context understand what you do in one read?
- **Absence of jargon and buzzwords** — "AI-powered," "next-generation," and "seamless" tell them nothing
- **Specificity of target** — "enterprises" and "teams" are not customers; the specific person who uses your product is

If the description is vague, abstract, or full of adjectives, it signals one of two problems: either the product isn't real yet, or you're hedging because you're not sure what it is. Both are red flags at the top of the application.

## What a strong answer looks like

Strong 50-character descriptions share a simple structure: **\[product\] \[verb\] for \[specific buyer\]**. The verb is concrete — it names the literal thing the product does. The buyer is specific — not "companies" or "users" but the actual person or business type.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Use a concrete noun for the subject">
    Name the product or category — not "platform," not "solution." If it's software, say what kind. If it's an API, say API. If it's a mobile app, let the verb imply it.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Choose a verb that names the action">
    "Automates," "tracks," "books," "files," "manages," "generates," "monitors" — all strong. "Transforms," "enables," "powers," "leverages," "revolutionizes" — all weak. The test: could you literally watch the product do this thing?
  </Step>
  <Step title="Name the specific buyer or user">
    "Restaurant chains," "Shopify sellers," "construction subcontractors," "Series A startups" — these are buyers. "Enterprises," "SMBs," "teams," "businesses" — these are not. The more specific, the better, even within 50 characters.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Remove every adjective">
    Strip out "AI-powered," "automated," "smart," "next-gen," and every other modifier. What's left? If the remaining sentence still describes your product accurately, you're done. If it doesn't make sense without the adjectives, that's a sign you're hiding behind them.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Count the characters">
    The form rejects entries over 50 characters. Count carefully — spaces count too.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Strong vs. weak: real examples

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Weak: 'AI-powered enterprise workflow optimization'">
    **Character count:** 44 — technically fits, but fails on every other dimension.

    This answer is all adjectives and category words with no subject, no verb, and no buyer. "Workflow optimization" could describe dozens of different products in dozens of different industries. A YC partner reading this learns nothing about what you built or who it's for. The phrase "AI-powered" is doing zero work — everything is AI-powered now.

    **What it signals to a reviewer:** Fuzzy thinking, unclear positioning, or a product that hasn't found its niche yet.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Strong: 'Payroll software for restaurant chains'">
    **Character count:** 38 — comfortably under limit.

    Subject: payroll software. Verb: implied (processes payroll). Buyer: restaurant chains. A partner reads this and immediately knows the product category, the specific industry, and the size/type of customer. No adjectives. No hedging. No ambiguity.

    **What it signals to a reviewer:** Clear product thinking, focused ICP, confident positioning.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Weak: 'The operating system for modern logistics'">
    **Character count:** 43 — fits, but meaningless.

    "Operating system for X" is a common hedge. It sounds ambitious but describes nothing. What does a user actually do? What does the software literally manage? "Modern" is filler. This description could apply to a dozen companies in the logistics space.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Strong: 'Automates SOC2 compliance audits for startups'">
    **Character count:** 46 — under limit.

    Verb: automates. Object: SOC2 compliance audits (extremely specific). Buyer: startups. A partner instantly knows what the product does, the specific pain it solves, and who buys it. No credentials for the founders needed — the description itself implies expertise in the domain.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## The most common mistakes

<Warning>
  **Starting with "We"** — The description is about the product, not the founders. "We help enterprises..." shifts the subject away from the product. Rewrite starting with the product or the action.
</Warning>

<Warning>
  **Using category words as the full answer** — "B2B SaaS marketplace platform," "vertical AI solution," "enterprise workflow tool." These phrases could describe thousands of companies. If your description could apply to a competitor, it's not specific enough.
</Warning>

<Warning>
  **Writing a mission statement** — "Empowering small businesses to compete with the Fortune 500" is an aspiration, not a description. YC wants to know what the product *does*, not what you hope it *means*.
</Warning>

<Warning>
  **Buzzword soup** — "AI-driven," "next-generation," "seamless," "holistic," "transformative." These are filler. Remove them all. If the description only makes sense with the adjectives in it, the underlying noun-verb-buyer structure isn't there yet.
</Warning>

<Warning>
  **"The Stripe/Airbnb for X"** — Analogies can be useful supplements in a longer answer, but they cannot replace a direct description. "The Stripe for healthcare" doesn't tell a reviewer what your product actually does. Add the literal description.
</Warning>

## The coach's probing question

When you're stuck or you think you've got a strong answer, run it through this test:

> **"What does your software actually do? Name the thing it does and who it does it for — in one plain sentence."**

Write the answer to that question out loud without worrying about character count. Then compress it. You'll almost always arrive at a cleaner 50-character version by starting from the plain-language answer rather than trying to craft the constrained version directly.

Additional pressure-test questions:
- "Remove every adjective from your description. Is it still accurate?"
- "Could this description apply to three of your competitors? If so, what would make it yours specifically?"
- "Is the buyer named? Is it a real specific category of person or company, or a placeholder word?"

<Tip>
  Say it out loud to someone who has never heard of your company. If their first response is "what does that mean?" or "like, what kind of compliance?" — rewrite it. The test isn't whether *you* understand it. It's whether a stranger understands it instantly.
</Tip>

## Consistency check

Your 50-character description and your full product description (the next question) must describe the same product. A common mistake is writing a tight, specific one-liner and then opening up the full product description into a much broader "platform" that the one-liner doesn't match. If those two answers describe different products, reviewers notice — and it signals positioning confusion.

Write the one-liner first. Then verify that every sentence in your full product description is consistent with it.
