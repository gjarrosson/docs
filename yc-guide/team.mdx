The team section is about demonstrating why this specific group of people is the right one to solve this specific problem — not just that you have the credentials to be taken seriously. YC funds teams that can build and sell, that have an unfair advantage from lived experience, and that are fully committed to making this work. The bar for the team section isn't to impress reviewers with résumé highlights — it's to show evidence of shipping, a credible reason why you understand this problem better than anyone else, and a team structure that covers the essential dimensions of building a company. Credentials establish context. What you've built establishes capability.

## The exact questions

> 1. **Who writes code, or does other technical work on your product? Was any of it done by a non-founder?**
> 2. **Are you looking for a cofounder?**

## What YC is evaluating

YC is checking for three things in this section:

**Technical coverage** — Is there at least one founder who writes production code? A founding team where no one builds the product is a structural risk. Technical decisions depend on contractors who have no equity and can walk away. YC views this as a serious gap that's hard to fix.

**Founder-market fit** — Does the team have an unfair advantage from lived experience? The strongest applications have at least one founder who has personally lived the problem — not just researched it, not just worked adjacent to it, but felt it directly and built a solution because no good one existed.

**Team completeness** — Do the founders cover building and selling? Is there clear role division, or is everyone doing everything (and therefore no one is accountable for anything)?

## The technical question: what a strong answer looks like

Name the founder who writes code. Describe what they specifically built in the current product — not their background, not their years of experience, but the specific features or systems they wrote.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Name the person and their role">
    "Jane (CTO) writes all of the production code" or "Both co-founders write code — I own the frontend and onboarding flow; my co-founder owns the backend and ML pipeline." Names and role division, not aggregate team stats.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Describe what they built — in this product">
    Not "she has 8 years of experience." What did she build for this company? "She shipped the MVP in 6 weeks and has deployed 3 major features since launch" is strong. "She has extensive full-stack experience" is not.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Show prior shipping history">
    What did the technical founder build before this company? A previous product with user or revenue numbers attached is the gold standard: "He built [Product], which reached 5K users before we shut it down to focus here." This proves they can ship, not just that they know how to code.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Address non-founder technical work directly">
    If contractors or employees have contributed, say so and explain what they did and why. Hiding it looks worse than disclosing it. "We used a contractor to build the initial design prototype; all production code since then is ours" is credible and honest.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Founder-market fit: the backstory that matters

Founder-market fit is the specific experience or lived-pain story that makes you better positioned to solve this problem than anyone else who might try. Asserting it isn't enough. You need to demonstrate it with a story that only you could tell.

The structure that works is simple: **"I was [role] at [place]. I personally experienced [specific problem]. Here's what it cost or how it felt. That's why I built this."**

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Weak: credentials without a backstory">
    > Our team has 20+ years of combined experience in enterprise software and security. Our CEO has an MBA from Wharton and previously worked at McKinsey. Our CTO has a computer science degree from MIT and has built software at Fortune 500 companies.

    **What's wrong:** No product has been built. No problem has been lived. This is a collection of institutional affiliations with no evidence of execution capability or domain understanding. The phrase "20+ years of combined experience" is the oldest hedge in the startup world — it can mean two people who spent 10 years each doing completely unrelated work.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Strong: lived experience plus proof of building">
    > I (CEO) ran the compliance function at [company] for 2 years — I was the person submitting evidence to auditors, the person who failed audits, and the person who rebuilt the program from scratch. My co-founder (CTO) built the internal compliance tooling at [company] that handled 4 SOC2 audits. She's written production code for 8 years. We've been working together for 6 months, both full-time since January. Our current product is entirely her code — she shipped the MVP in 6 weeks.

    **What's right:** Two specific backstories with specific roles and specific experiences. Clear role division — one person knows the domain from living it, the other has demonstrated they can build. Evidence of shipping: a 6-week MVP timeline. Full-time commitment with a specific date. Working history together.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Common mistakes

<Warning>
  **"Deep expertise in X" without specific evidence** — Expertise is a conclusion, not evidence. What did you do in that domain? What did you build, learn, or discover? Replace the assertion with the story.
</Warning>

<Warning>
  **Credentials-only profiles** — "10 years at Google" tells a reviewer nothing about whether you can build a startup. What did you ship at Google? What did you learn? Did any of it give you an insight that your competitors don't have?
</Warning>

<Warning>
  **"We all contribute to the product"** — This phrase almost always means roles are undefined and no one is clearly accountable for anything. Name who does what. Clarity about role division is a signal of team maturity, not limitation.
</Warning>

<Warning>
  **Listing titles without building evidence** — "Our CTO has extensive full-stack experience and is responsible for all technical decisions" without a single named thing they've built is not a strong answer. What did they ship, and what happened?
</Warning>

<Warning>
  **"We're looking for a technical co-founder"** — If no one on the team writes code, this is the most important thing in your application to address directly. Hiding it or soft-pedaling it looks worse than saying it plainly. See the cofounder section below.
</Warning>

## The cofounder question

If you're actively looking for a cofounder, be direct about it. Explain what gap you're trying to fill and why the current team can still make meaningful progress without that person.

<Note>
  Saying "yes, we're looking for a technical co-founder — here's what we've built so far without one and here's the gap we're trying to fill" is substantially more credible than pretending the team is complete when it isn't. YC has funded companies where a cofounder was still being recruited. They've also passed on companies that obscured a critical gap in the team section. Honesty reads better than hedging.
</Note>

If you're not looking for a cofounder, explain why the current team is sufficient — not just "we work well together," but a specific account of what each person covers and why those dimensions are the ones that matter for where you are right now.

## The pressure-test questions for every founder

Before you finalize the team section, answer these for every founder on the team:

<Tip>
  For every founder, ask: **"What is the most relevant thing this person has built or done in this specific problem space? What did they learn from it that makes them better at this than someone starting from scratch?"**

  If you can't answer that question with a specific story and a specific outcome, the team section isn't ready. You don't need an impressive résumé — you need a credible reason why this person, in particular, is the right one for this problem.
</Tip>

For the technical founder specifically:
- What did they write in this product in the last 30 days?
- What's the last product they shipped before this company, and what did it do?
- Are they full-time? If not, when does that change and why?

For the domain/business founder:
- When did they personally experience the problem this product solves?
- What gives them access to customers that competitors don't have?
- What did they learn from their prior role that the product is built on?

## Solo founders

Being a solo founder is not disqualifying, but it raises the bar on execution evidence. YC wants to see that you can move fast without a co-founder compensating for gaps. The strongest solo founder applications show:

- A clear track record of building and shipping independently
- Velocity that signals you don't need a team to make progress: "I launched in February, have 3 paying customers, and shipped 4 major features in 8 weeks"
- A specific plan for what kind of co-founder or first hire you'd bring on and why you haven't yet
