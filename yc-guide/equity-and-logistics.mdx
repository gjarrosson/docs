The equity, location, and curious sections of the YC application are less about narrative and more about accurate, honest information delivered cleanly. These aren't questions where a compelling story wins you points — they're hygiene checks and logistics questions. What wins points here is giving complete, direct answers with no ambiguity and no surprises. A clean answer signals maturity. A vague or evasive one creates doubt that spreads to the rest of your application.

---

## Equity Questions

These three questions collectively check for cap table issues that could complicate YC's investment. YC invests via a standard SAFE at a fixed valuation cap. Anything that makes that harder — messy cap tables, complex prior terms, founder equity without vesting — creates friction. Answer each one directly.

### "Have you formed ANY legal entity yet?"

State yes or no. If yes, name the entity type, jurisdiction, and the date of incorporation.

**Strong answer:** "Yes — Delaware C-Corp, incorporated March 2024. 4-year vesting with a 1-year cliff on all founder equity, started vesting January 2024. Cap table is clean: 60/40 founder split, no outside investors."

**Weak answer:** "We plan to incorporate before the batch starts."

If you haven't incorporated yet, say so clearly — but be aware that YC expects companies to be incorporated before the batch. If you're not, explain your timeline.

<Note>
  If you haven't set up vesting on founder equity yet, do it before you submit. Founders without vesting schedules are a red flag to sophisticated investors: if one founder leaves after six months, they could walk away with a large percentage of the company. YC will notice, and it will create friction. Set up vesting, then answer this question accurately.
</Note>

### "Have you taken any investment yet?"

Be specific: name the total amount raised, the instrument type (SAFE, priced round, convertible note), the investors (named or described — "a $250K pre-seed SAFE from two angel investors in our network"), and the key terms (valuation cap, discount rate, MFN clause if applicable).

If you haven't taken any investment, say so directly: "No — bootstrapped to date."

**Strong answer:** "Yes — $300K on a SAFE at a $5M cap, no discount. Two angel investors. Cap table is two founders plus the two angels on the SAFE. No other obligations or encumbrances."

**Weak answer:** "We have verbal commitments from investors but nothing signed."

<Warning>
  Verbal commitments are not investments. Only signed documents count. Don't list verbal agreements as investment — it creates an impression of traction you don't actually have, and it will surface as inaccurate the moment anyone looks closely.
</Warning>

### "Are you currently fundraising?"

Answer directly: yes or no. If yes, state the round size, how much is committed (signed), and your timeline.

YC does not penalize you for fundraising in parallel. What they want to know is where things stand — and whether anything in your current raise might create complications (e.g., a priced round that would make the YC SAFE structurally awkward).

**Strong answer:** "Yes — we're raising a $500K pre-seed. $150K is committed on SAFEs. We plan to close the round after the YC decision."

<Note>
  If you've taken investment at terms that might seem unusual — a very high valuation cap, a complex structure, or a priced round at an early stage — explain it briefly in this section. Surprises create doubt; brief explanations create context. A single sentence like "the $8M cap reflects an early commitment from a strategic investor in the space — we can explain the reasoning" is far better than leaving a reviewer to wonder.
</Note>

## Location Questions

### "Where do you live now, and where would the company be based after YC?"

Answer in the format YC specifies: **City A, Country A / City B, Country B**. If founders are in different cities, name each.

This question is asking two things: where you are now, and whether you'll be in San Francisco for the batch. YC's batch is in person in SF. If you'll be there, say so directly.

**Example:** "New York, USA / San Francisco, USA (for batch)"

**Example (international):** "Berlin, Germany / San Francisco, USA (for batch; we'll relocate for the duration)"

### "Explain your decision regarding location."

This is not an invitation to write an essay about remote work as a philosophy. It's asking for a brief, direct explanation of why your current location makes sense for the business — or what your plan is to be in SF for the batch.

<Steps>
  <Step title="If you're already in San Francisco or relocating for the batch">
    Say so clearly. "We're in SF and will be here for the full batch" is a complete answer. No elaboration needed.
  </Step>
  <Step title="If you're outside the US">
    Acknowledge the visa situation and show you've done the research. YC has handled hundreds of international founders and will help, but they need to see that you're aware of what's required and have a realistic plan.

    **Example:** "We're in Berlin. We've researched the O-1 visa timeline and have connected with an immigration attorney. We're prepared to move to SF for the batch duration and plan to evaluate whether to stay in the US afterward based on where our customers are."
  </Step>
  <Step title="If you're staying in a different city for strategic reasons">
    This is the one case where a short explanation earns its place. If your location is genuinely important to the business — you're near your first cluster of customers, you're building in a market that requires physical presence, you're in a regulatory environment that's relevant — say so specifically.

    **Weak:** "We believe remote work is the future and our team is distributed."

    **Strong:** "We're in Houston because our first 8 customers are mid-market oil and gas companies. All of our initial sales have happened through in-person relationships. We'll be in SF for the batch and plan to evaluate relocation after we've closed our first 20 customers."
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Curious Section

These are the lowest-stakes questions in the application. YC is checking for basic authenticity, not making major decisions. Answer genuinely and briefly — these should be two or three sentences, not paragraphs.

### "What convinced you to apply to Y Combinator?"

Avoid generic answers. "YC is the best accelerator in the world" adds nothing and reads as a form answer. "We need the funding" is honest but signals you're optimizing for the check rather than the program.

Strong answers name something specific about YC that matters for your situation: the alumni network in your vertical, the batch experience at your stage, the specific advice from YC essays that shaped how you think, or a YC company or founder whose story influenced your decision.

**Weak:** "YC is the most prestigious accelerator and we want to be part of that network."

**Strong:** "I've been following YC since reading Paul Graham's essays in 2018. The 'do things that don't scale' principle is something we've applied directly — our first 8 customers came from manually onboarding them ourselves. More specifically, we want the batch experience: 3 months of being surrounded by founders at exactly our stage, with partners who have pattern-matched on what we're going through."

<Tip>
  A specific personal story reads more authentically than a generic one. If you met a YC alum who told you something that changed how you think, say so. If a specific essay reshaped your approach to sales or building, name it. Specificity signals genuine engagement with the program — not just the prestige.
</Tip>

### "How did you hear about Y Combinator?"

This is a logistics question for YC's own tracking. Answer honestly and directly.

**Examples:**
- "Through a YC alum at [company] who told us to apply."
- "Hacker News — I've been reading it for 4 years."
- "A friend in the [batch] batch recommended it."
- "Through the YC Startup School program."

No elaboration needed. One sentence is right.

## Quick Reference: What to Check Before Submitting

<Accordion title="Equity checklist">
  Before you submit, make sure you can answer each of these cleanly:

  - [ ] Entity type, state/country, and incorporation date
  - [ ] Founder equity percentages with vesting schedules (4-year, 1-year cliff is standard)
  - [ ] Full list of any outside investors with instrument type and key terms
  - [ ] Any existing SAFEs, convertible notes, or equity agreements noted with caps/discounts
  - [ ] No unsigned "verbal commitments" listed as investment
  - [ ] Any unusual terms briefly explained (not hidden)
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Location checklist">
  - [ ] Current city/country for each founder named explicitly
  - [ ] Post-YC location stated clearly, with "San Francisco, USA" if you'll be there for the batch
  - [ ] If international: visa plan acknowledged and in progress
  - [ ] If staying elsewhere: a specific, strategic reason tied to the business — not remote-work philosophy
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Curious section checklist">
  - [ ] "What convinced you" has a specific answer, not a generic one
  - [ ] "How did you hear" is answered honestly in one sentence
  - [ ] Neither answer is longer than 3–4 sentences
</Accordion>
