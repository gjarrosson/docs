This is your chance to show that you've built something real and that you understand exactly how it works. Most founders under-describe their product and over-describe the problem — they write three paragraphs about how painful the status quo is and then add one vague sentence about their solution. YC reviewers notice this immediately, and they interpret it as a sign that the product isn't real yet or that the founders aren't confident in what they've built. Your job in this answer is to make the product concrete: describe what a user actually does in it, what exists right now versus what's on the roadmap, and why the current version is valuable enough to matter.

## The exact question

> **What is your company going to make? Please describe your product and what it does or will do.**

## What YC is evaluating

YC uses this answer to calibrate three things simultaneously:

- **Is the product real?** Can you describe it in present tense — what it does today, not just what it will do someday?
- **Do you understand your users?** Can you describe a specific workflow that a specific person goes through when they use it?
- **Is your vision coherent?** Does the product you describe match the 50-character description you wrote above, and does it solve the problem you'll describe later in the application?

The reviewers are looking for evidence of clarity, not ambition. A product described with precision signals that you've built it, talked to users, and watched them use it. A product described in abstract, aspirational language signals the opposite — that the product is still mostly an idea.

## What a strong answer looks like

Strong product descriptions are grounded in the user's experience, not the founder's vision. They describe a workflow. They name what's real today. They're honest about what's next.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Open with what the product literally is">
    State the form factor and core category in the first sentence. Is it a web app? A mobile app? An API? A hardware device? A browser extension? Name it. "We built a SaaS tool that..." or "We built a mobile app for..." or "We built an API that..." — all strong openings. "We're building a platform that leverages..." — weak.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Describe the core user workflow">
    Walk through what a user does on day one. What do they connect, configure, or enter? What does the product do with that input? What do they see or receive on the other side? This is the most important part of your answer. If you can describe a complete day-one workflow in two or three sentences, you're almost certainly describing a real, scoped product.
  </Step>
  <Step title="State the outcome in concrete terms">
    What changes for the user after they use your product? Name the specific thing that is better, faster, cheaper, or eliminated. Use numbers when you have them — "audits that took 3 months now take 2 weeks" is far stronger than "dramatically reduces audit time."
  </Step>
  <Step title="Say what exists today">
    Be explicit about what is built, deployed, and usable right now. Use present tense for what's running. Don't let reviewers wonder whether the product is live or still in Figma.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Describe what's next — briefly">
    One to two sentences on the roadmap is enough. Tie it to what you've learned from current users, not to an abstract feature wishlist. "We're adding X because our current users are asking for it and it's the next step in the workflow" is credible. A five-bullet roadmap that bears no connection to user feedback is not.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Before and after: what the difference looks like

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Weak: platform language with no workflow">
    > We are building a platform that leverages AI to enable companies to streamline their compliance workflows and reduce operational overhead, ultimately allowing them to focus on their core business.

    **What's wrong:** No subject other than "companies." No specific workflow described. "Streamline" and "reduce operational overhead" say nothing specific. No mention of what exists today. "Ultimately allowing them to focus on their core business" is a filler phrase that could appear in any application for any product. A reviewer learns nothing about the actual product from this paragraph.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Strong: specific workflow, real product, present tense">
    > We built a SaaS tool that automates evidence collection for SOC2 audits. When a company connects their AWS, GitHub, and Okta accounts, we continuously collect the screenshots, logs, and access records that auditors require. Audits that took 3 months of manual work now take 2 weeks. We launched in January and have 4 paying customers at $500/month.

    **What's right:** Named product type (SaaS tool). Named the specific workflow (connecting three specific integrations, collecting three specific types of evidence). Stated the outcome with numbers (3 months → 2 weeks). Confirmed the product is live with a launch date and paying customers. Every sentence adds information that couldn't have been written about a different product.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## The walk-through test

Before you finalize your product description, run it through this question:

> **"If I were a new user, what would I actually do in this product on day one? Walk me through the exact steps."**

If you can't answer that question with specific steps — what you click, what you enter, what the product does, what you see when it's done — your product description isn't ready. The walk-through test reveals whether you're describing a real product or a product concept. It also reveals whether your answer actually describes what you've built or what you're planning to build.

Additional pressure tests:
- "What is the single most important feature — the one thing that makes this useful at all, without which there's no product?"
- "If you ran a 60-second demo right now, what would you show and in what order?"
- "Is there anything in this description that doesn't exist yet? If so, have you labeled it clearly as what you're building next?"

## Common mistakes

<Warning>
  **Describing the problem more than the product** — Three paragraphs on the pain and one sentence on the solution is the most common structural failure. Your answer should spend more words on the product than on the context.
</Warning>

<Warning>
  **Confusing features with the core product** — Listing five features without explaining the core workflow that makes any of them valuable is not a product description. Identify the single thing your product does that is the reason a user would pay for it, and build your description around that.
</Warning>

<Warning>
  **All roadmap, no present** — "We will build X, then add Y, then integrate with Z" with nothing about what exists today reads as: the product isn't built yet. If you're pre-product, say so clearly and describe what you're building. But if you have something built, describe what's there now before you describe what's coming.
</Warning>

<Warning>
  **Contradicting your 50-character description** — If your one-liner says "bookkeeping software for Shopify sellers" but your product description describes a general-purpose finance platform, that's a contradiction. Reviewers notice it and flag it as positioning confusion. Both answers should describe the same product.
</Warning>

<Warning>
  **Abstract enabling language** — "Our platform enables teams to collaborate more effectively on X" is not a product description. It's a value proposition dressed up as a description. "Teams open a shared dashboard, import their X data, and run our automated analysis to get a prioritized list of Y" is a product description.
</Warning>

<Note>
  If you're pre-product, say so clearly and describe what you're building in concrete terms. "We're building a web app that lets restaurant managers schedule shifts by entering their weekly forecast — it generates a draft schedule in under a minute" is fine. "We're exploring the intersection of AI and workflow automation for the food service industry" is not. Pre-product companies get into YC. Vague companies do not.
</Note>
